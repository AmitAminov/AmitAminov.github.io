About

I started in physics (B.Sc., Bar-Ilan’s program for youth gifted in mathematics) because I wanted models that are actually accountable to reality. That instinct carried into military operations research — first leading an OR and data-science team in Unit 8200, then in the IAF’s Operations Research Branch, work recognized with the Israel Defense Prize. Today, in my research M.Sc. at the Hebrew University, I study the strongest form of accountability I know: machine learning for formal reasoning — fine-tuning LLMs to prove theorems in Lean, where a verifier, not a leaderboard, decides what counts.

Research focus

The through-line is skepticism about claims: chronological splits instead of shuffled ones, frequency-table baselines instead of only model-vs-model comparisons, artifact-scored reproductions instead of agent self-reports, and — ultimately — proofs checked by a verifier.

My current work fine-tunes LLMs for theorem proving in Lean 4 using GRPO, with the Lean verifier itself supplying the reward signal: a proof either checks or it doesn’t, so the model cannot be rewarded for sounding right. Training runs on a university GPU cluster against Mathlib-scale tactic data, with the goal-blind frequency baselines from Generated Formal Theorem Proofs as the floor any learned policy has to clear. The goal is a prover whose reported gains survive the same skepticism the rest of this page applies to everything else.
